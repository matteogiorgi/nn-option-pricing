\chapter{Conclusions and Future Work}

\section{Conclusions}

The final experiment confirms that a feed-forward neural network can approximate the Black-Scholes pricing function for European call options with high accuracy in a controlled synthetic environment.
With 100,000 synthetic contracts, the network achieves an MAE of 0.04290, an RMSE of 0.06208, and an $R^2$ of 0.999993 on the held-out test set.
These results answer the research question positively within the assumptions and parameter ranges considered in the project.

The comparison with Monte Carlo clarifies the role of the neural network.
Monte Carlo is a classical numerical estimator and remains useful as a benchmark, while the trained neural network acts as a deterministic pricing surrogate.
The runtime benchmark further supports the surrogate interpretation: after training, neural inference is substantially faster than Monte Carlo simulation for repeated pricing queries.

The additional SVR experiment provides a classical machine learning reference point on reduced datasets.
The noisy-target experiments show that both the neural network and the SVR baseline remain accurate under controlled label perturbations, although approximation quality decreases as the noise level increases.

\section{Limitations}

The main limitations are:

\begin{itemize}
    \item the data are synthetic and generated under Black-Scholes assumptions;
    \item only European call options are considered;
    \item the target pricing function is known analytically;
    \item noisy targets are controlled perturbations and should not be interpreted as real market prices;
    \item generalization outside the sampled parameter ranges is not guaranteed.
\end{itemize}

These limitations are not flaws in the experiment, but boundaries of the research question.
The project deliberately studies a controlled approximation problem before moving to more complex financial settings.

\section{Future Extensions}

Natural future extensions include:

\begin{itemize}
    \item compare additional neural network architectures beyond the selected MLP;
    \item tune and evaluate further classical machine learning baselines on controlled reduced datasets;
    \item study the error in specific regions of the parameter space, for instance deep in-the-money or out-of-the-money options;
    \item extend the framework to stochastic volatility models such as Heston;
    \item price more complex derivatives such as basket options or American options;
    \item estimate Greeks using either automatic differentiation or finite differences;
    \item investigate real or pseudo-real option data while explicitly changing the research question to market-price modeling.
\end{itemize}

These extensions would generalize the approach beyond the Black-Scholes benchmark while preserving the experimental discipline developed in the current project.
