\chapter{Experimental Results}

\section{Reading Guide}

This chapter reports the empirical results obtained from the experiments described in the previous chapters.
The main reference target is always the analytical Black-Scholes price $C_{BS}$.
When a model produces an estimate $\hat{C}$, the reported errors measure the distance between $\hat{C}$ and $C_{BS}$ on held-out observations.

The experiments are organized into four groups:

\begin{itemize}
    \item the final neural-network experiment, trained on 100,000 synthetic options;
    \item the Monte Carlo and runtime comparisons, used as quantitative benchmarks;
    \item intermediate model-selection experiments, used to justify moneyness and SiLU;
    \item robustness and classical machine-learning checks, based on noisy targets and SVR.
\end{itemize}
The different experiments do not all have the same computational scale.
The final neural network is the main experiment, while SVR benchmarks are intentionally run on reduced datasets because kernel methods scale less favorably with the number of training samples.

\begin{quote}
    The main empirical conclusion is not that the neural network replaces the analytical Black-Scholes formula.
    Rather, the experiment shows that a supervised neural network can learn the Black-Scholes pricing map very accurately and can act as a fast pricing surrogate after training.
\end{quote}

\section{Final Neural Network Performance}

The selected final model uses the engineered moneyness feature and SiLU activations.
It is trained on 100,000 synthetic contracts generated under the Black-Scholes framework, with a 70\%/15\%/15\% train-validation-test split.
The final supervised learning map is:

\begin{equation}
    (S_0, K, T, r, \sigma, S_0/K)
    \mapsto
    \hat{C}_{NN}.
\end{equation}

The four metrics in Table~\ref{tab:nn-final-metrics} evaluate complementary aspects of the approximation:
absolute error, squared-error sensitivity, explained variance, and relative error on economically meaningful prices.

\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \begin{tabular}{lr}
        \toprule
        Metric             & Value    \\
        \midrule
        MAE                & 0.04290  \\
        RMSE               & 0.06208  \\
        $R^2$              & 0.999993 \\
        MAPE, prices $> 1$ & 0.4803\% \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
    \caption{Final neural network metrics against analytical Black-Scholes prices.}
    \label{tab:nn-final-metrics}
\end{table}

The error levels are small in absolute price units.
The coefficient of determination is very close to one, which means that almost all variability in the analytical Black-Scholes prices over the sampled domain is reproduced by the neural network.
The MAPE value, computed only for options whose theoretical price is greater than 1, confirms that the relative error is also low in economically meaningful price regions.

Figure~\ref{fig:final-loss} shows that the optimization process is stable.
The validation curve follows the training curve closely, which suggests that the model is not simply memorizing the training set.
This is important because the goal is to approximate the pricing function on unseen parameter combinations, not only to fit the sampled contracts.

\section{Monte Carlo Comparison}

Monte Carlo pricing is evaluated against the same analytical Black-Scholes reference, but on a deterministic subset of 512 test options for computational reasons.
Each option is priced using 50,000 terminal samples.
The comparison is therefore a numerical benchmark rather than a fully symmetric replacement for the neural-network test-set evaluation.

\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \begin{tabular}{lr}
        \toprule
        Metric             & Value    \\
        \midrule
        MAE                & 0.08882  \\
        RMSE               & 0.15790  \\
        $R^2$              & 0.999951 \\
        MAPE, prices $> 1$ & 0.6483\% \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
    \caption{Final Monte Carlo metrics against analytical Black-Scholes prices.}
    \label{tab:mc-final-metrics}
\end{table}

The Monte Carlo benchmark also reaches high accuracy, as expected with a large number of paths.
However, Monte Carlo remains a stochastic estimator:

\begin{equation}
    \hat{C}_{MC}
    =
    C_{BS}
    +
    \varepsilon_{MC},
    \qquad
    \varepsilon_{MC} = O_P(M^{-1/2}),
\end{equation}

where $M$ is the number of simulated paths.
Increasing $M$ reduces sampling error, but also increases runtime.
By contrast, once trained, the neural network produces deterministic forward-pass predictions.

\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \begin{tabular}{lrrrr}
        \toprule
        Method & MAE & RMSE & $R^2$ & MAPE, prices $> 1$ \\
        \midrule
        Neural network & 0.04290 & 0.06208 & 0.999993 & 0.4803\% \\
        Monte Carlo    & 0.08882 & 0.15790 & 0.999951 & 0.6483\% \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
    \caption{Summary comparison of the final neural network and Monte Carlo benchmark.}
    \label{tab:nn-mc-summary}
\end{table}

The side-by-side comparison in Table~\ref{tab:nn-mc-summary} should be interpreted with care because the two methods are evaluated on different test-set sizes.
Nevertheless, it shows that the neural network surrogate reaches at least the same order of accuracy as the Monte Carlo benchmark in this controlled setting.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \maybeincludegraphics[width=0.72\linewidth]{figures/nn_vs_monte_carlo_metrics.png}
    \caption{Final neural-network and Monte Carlo errors against analytical Black-Scholes prices.}
    \label{fig:nn-vs-mc-metrics}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:nn-vs-mc-metrics} makes the scale of the error differences easier to compare across metrics.
The neural-network bars are lower for all reported error metrics, while the interpretation remains tied to the different evaluation protocols described above.

\section{Feature Engineering and Activation Function Results}

Before selecting the final configuration, intermediate experiments were used to test two design choices:
adding the moneyness feature and changing the hidden-layer activation function.
These experiments use 50,000 synthetic contracts and 100 training epochs.

\subsection{Moneyness Feature}

The moneyness experiment compares the base feature set

\begin{equation}
    (S_0, K, T, r, \sigma)
\end{equation}

with the engineered feature set

\begin{equation}
    (S_0, K, T, r, \sigma, S_0/K).
\end{equation}

Table~\ref{tab:moneyness-results} isolates the effect of adding this financial ratio while keeping the activation function fixed.

\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \begin{tabular}{lrrrr}
        \toprule
        Experiment & MAE & RMSE & $R^2$ & MAPE, prices $> 1$ \\
        \midrule
        Base + ReLU             & 0.17269 & 0.22973 & 0.999904 & 1.8687\% \\
        Moneyness + ReLU        & 0.15417 & 0.20930 & 0.999920 & 1.6670\% \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
    \caption{Intermediate feature-engineering experiment.}
    \label{tab:moneyness-results}
\end{table}

Adding moneyness reduces MAE by approximately 10.7\% in this controlled intermediate setting.
The feature does not add new theoretical information, because it is computed from $S_0$ and $K$.
However, it exposes a financially meaningful ratio directly to the model, which can simplify the regression problem.

\subsection{Activation Functions}

The activation-function experiment compares ReLU, Tanh, and SiLU using the same base feature set.
The three activation choices lead to the metrics in Table~\ref{tab:activation-results}.

\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \begin{tabular}{lrrrr}
        \toprule
        Experiment & MAE & RMSE & $R^2$ & MAPE, prices $> 1$ \\
        \midrule
        Base + ReLU & 0.17269 & 0.22973 & 0.999904 & 1.8687\% \\
        Base + Tanh & 0.11316 & 0.16541 & 0.999950 & 1.2878\% \\
        Base + SiLU & 0.10775 & 0.15817 & 0.999954 & 1.2488\% \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
    \caption{Intermediate activation-function experiment.}
    \label{tab:activation-results}
\end{table}

Both smooth activations outperform ReLU in this setting, with SiLU giving the lowest MAE and RMSE.
This is consistent with the target being a smooth analytical pricing function over the sampled parameter domain.

\subsection{Combined Configuration}

The strongest intermediate candidate combines the moneyness feature with SiLU activations.
Table~\ref{tab:combined-results} places this combined setup alongside the other intermediate configurations.

\begin{table}[H]

It does not dominate every metric: the best MAPE in this table is obtained by Base + SiLU.
For the final run, the combined configuration was selected because MAE and RMSE are the primary absolute-error metrics in price units.

\section{Runtime Results}

The runtime benchmark separates the initial training cost from pricing time after training.
This distinction matters because a neural network is expensive to train once, but cheap to evaluate many times afterwards.

The benchmark uses the improved neural configuration, 50,000 synthetic training samples, 100 epochs, and 1,000 pricing queries.
The Monte Carlo benchmark uses 20,000 paths.
The timing numbers in Table~\ref{tab:runtime-benchmark} separate vectorized analytical pricing, learned inference, and simulation-based pricing.

\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \begin{tabular}{lrr}
        \toprule
        Method                    & Mean time for 1,000 options & Mean options/s \\
        \midrule
        Black-Scholes formula     & 0.00091 s                   & 1,101,094.82   \\
        Neural network inference  & 0.03190 s                   & 31,350.77      \\
        Monte Carlo, 20,000 paths & 1.28235 s                   & 779.82         \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
    \caption{Runtime benchmark for analytical, neural-network, and Monte Carlo pricing.}
    \label{tab:runtime-benchmark}
\end{table}

The analytical formula is the fastest method when it is available.
The more relevant surrogate-model comparison is therefore between neural-network inference and Monte Carlo simulation.
In this benchmark, after training, neural inference is approximately 40 times faster than Monte Carlo for the same 1,000 pricing queries.
The trade-off is the initial training cost, which was approximately 359 seconds in the benchmark environment.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \maybeincludegraphics[width=0.72\linewidth]{figures/runtime_comparison.png}
    \caption{Mean pricing time for 1,000 options.}
    \label{fig:runtime-comparison}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:runtime-comparison} uses a logarithmic vertical axis because the three pricing methods operate on very different time scales.
This makes the inference-time advantage over Monte Carlo visible without hiding the fact that the analytical Black-Scholes formula remains the fastest method in this specific setting.

\section{SVR Benchmark Results}

Support Vector Regression provides an additional classical machine-learning baseline.
The experiment is repeated over three random seeds, using 5,000 synthetic contracts per run and the same engineered moneyness feature used by the final neural network.
Input features and target prices are standardized before fitting the SVR, and predictions are transformed back to price units before computing metrics.

\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \begin{tabular}{lrr}
        \toprule
        Metric             & Mean      & Standard deviation \\
        \midrule
        MAE                & 0.15088   & 0.00732            \\
        RMSE               & 0.23100   & 0.00592            \\
        $R^2$              & 0.999905  & 0.000005           \\
        MAPE, prices $> 1$ & 1.8253\%  & 0.2422\%           \\
        Fit time           & 12.3198 s & 0.7977 s           \\
        Prediction time    & 0.0301 s  & 0.0029 s           \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
    \caption{Reduced-scale SVR benchmark aggregated over three random seeds.}
    \label{tab:svr-benchmark}
\end{table}

The SVR baseline approximates the Black-Scholes function well on reduced datasets, as shown by its high $R^2$.
However, its absolute error is higher than that of the final neural network configuration.
Because the SVR experiment uses smaller datasets, this result should be read as a classical baseline check rather than as a direct large-scale competition.

\section{Noisy Target Robustness Results}

The noisy-target experiment tests whether the neural network can still recover the clean analytical pricing function when the training labels are perturbed.
For each clean price $C_{BS}$, the training target is:

\begin{equation}
    \tilde{C}
    =
    \max\left(
        C_{BS} + \epsilon,
        0
    \right),
    \qquad
    \epsilon
    \sim
    \mathcal{N}\left(0, \left[\alpha \max(C_{BS}, 1)\right]^2\right),
\end{equation}

where $\alpha$ is the noise level.
The model is trained on $\tilde{C}$ but evaluated against the clean $C_{BS}$.
This keeps the experiment aligned with the original research question while adding controlled label noise.
The 0\% row in Table~\ref{tab:noisy-targets} is the clean-label control for this robustness setup, not a repetition of the final 100,000-sample experiment.

\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \begin{tabular}{lrrrr}
        \toprule
        Noise level & MAE     & RMSE    & $R^2$    & MAPE, prices $> 1$ \\
        \midrule
        0\%         & 0.10254 & 0.15169 & 0.999958 & 1.3213\%           \\
        1\%         & 0.11084 & 0.16105 & 0.999953 & 1.3960\%           \\
        5\%         & 0.17827 & 0.24167 & 0.999893 & 2.0943\%           \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
    \caption{Noisy-target neural-network results evaluated against clean Black-Scholes prices.}
    \label{tab:noisy-targets}
\end{table}

Table~\ref{tab:noisy-targets} shows the expected degradation as label noise increases.
The model remains accurate under 1\% noise, while 5\% noise produces a clearer increase in both MAE and RMSE.
This supports a cautious robustness interpretation: the neural network can partially smooth noisy labels, but noisy supervision still reduces approximation quality.

The same noisy-target experiment is repeated with the reduced-scale SVR baseline.
Table~\ref{tab:noisy-svr-targets} gives the corresponding SVR behavior under the same label-noise levels.

\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \begin{tabular}{lrrrr}
        \toprule
        Noise level & MAE     & RMSE    & $R^2$    & MAPE, prices $> 1$ \\
        \midrule
        0\%         & 0.15088 & 0.23100 & 0.999905 & 1.8253\%           \\
        1\%         & 0.19284 & 0.30765 & 0.999830 & 1.8705\%           \\
        5\%         & 0.56185 & 1.05057 & 0.998025 & 3.4903\%           \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
    \caption{Noisy-target SVR benchmark evaluated against clean Black-Scholes prices.}
    \label{tab:noisy-svr-targets}
\end{table}

SVR remains accurate under mild label noise, but it degrades more strongly at the 5\% noise level.
This comparison is useful because it shows that the noisy-target effect is not specific to the neural-network implementation.
At the same time, the SVR experiment remains a reduced-scale benchmark and should not be interpreted as a replacement for the final neural-network experiment.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \maybeincludegraphics[width=0.72\linewidth]{figures/noisy_target_robustness.png}
    \caption{MAE against clean Black-Scholes prices under increasing label noise.}
    \label{fig:noisy-target-robustness}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:noisy-target-robustness} highlights the monotone degradation caused by increasing label noise.
The neural-network curve remains below the SVR curve in this experiment, but the comparison should be interpreted as a robustness reference rather than as a fully symmetric benchmark because the two models are evaluated at different computational scales.

\section{Error Analysis}

Aggregate metrics are necessary but not sufficient.
Two models with similar MAE may behave differently across moneyness, maturity, or volatility regions.
For this reason, the final experiment includes diagnostic plots of prediction errors.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \maybeincludegraphics[width=0.72\linewidth]{../results/final/figures/error_distribution.png}
    \caption{Distribution of neural network prediction errors.}
    \label{fig:final-error-distribution}
\end{figure}

The error distribution in Figure~\ref{fig:final-error-distribution} is concentrated near zero, which is consistent with the low absolute-error metrics of the final model.
This suggests that the final result is not only an artifact of aggregate averaging, but corresponds to generally small pointwise errors across the test set.
